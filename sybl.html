Sybl

Open simulation architecture: new practices in AI research, urbanism, and cyber-physical systems

In recent decades, architects, designers, scientists, and engineers have turned to computer simulation with the hope of designing more functional, sustainable, and compelling designs. Computer-aided-design grew from being only applied to the design of single elements to include large-scale complex systems such as factories, entire cities, robots, and autonomous vehicles. A multitude of simulation tools has been developed to model specific aspects of complex phenomena such as thermodynamics, climate, crowd behavior, structural integrity, economic and financial behavior. Yet numerous challenges remain. For example, although many factors influencing the design of complex systems are interdependent, they are often analyzed in isolation due to a lack of interoperable formats that enable the integration of various simulation engines together. A systems approach combining visual programming, state-of-the-art modeling with collaborative simulation techniques may help designers, engineers and scientists to combine their expertise to produce integrated complex systems models that support emerging paradigms such as co-generative design.

Discipline: computer simulations, CAD, digital-twin
Role: research, interface and software design
Team: Mark Wilcox, Mariia Fedorova
Research Partner: Strelka Media Institute
Year: 2019

In every paradigm shift there lie dangers and opportunities. While there are many risks involved in giving more autonomy to simulations and computer modeling to govern the design of physical space, there are also opportunities to fundamentally rethink how simulations are conceived, opening up new avenues for how we may co-design within a simulated environment. How can the autonomous vehicle optimize beyond the parameters set by their designers to also include the needs of the city in which they drive?

By rethinking simulations and their architecture, we can fundamentally alter the design outcome. Such transformations can be achieved not only by improving existing models but by building interfaces under which currently isolated machine learning models can begin to optimize for a common and interconnected design objective. As computer modeling becomes ever more pervasive, the design of the software architecture is also the design of physical space.
